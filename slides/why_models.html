How and Why to Use Models

Joseph Mullins

When You Don’t Need a Model

Causal Inference Without Deep Structure

  • Many questions take the form: “What is the effect of \(X\) on \(Y\)?”

  • Often answerable with simple statistical models of causality (Potential Outcomes, Generalized Roy)

  • No need to specify preferences, technology, or equilibrium

  • But the target parameter must be precisely defined

  • Key requirement: observed variation directly identifies the counterfactual of interest

  • Three examples from our prototype models below

Example: Social Security

  • Consider the life-cycle savings model with a social security system

  • The budget constraint: \[ c_{t} + a_{t+1}/(1 + r) \leq (1-\tau)y_{t} + \mathbf{1}\{t\geq 65\}b \]

  • Individuals receive benefit \(b\) after age 65 and pay proportional taxes \(\tau\)

  • Suppose eligibility is unexpectedly lowered from 65 to 60

  • Repeated cross-sections of consumption by age and cohort are available

Social Security: Target Parameter

  • Let \(t^*\) index cohorts by age at the time of the policy announcement

  • \(t^*\) indexes both a cohort and a treatment

  • Question: “What was the effect of this eligibility expansion on consumption at age \(t\) for cohort \(t^*\)?”

  • \(C_{t^*,t}(1)\): consumption under the announcement; \(C_{t^*,t}(0)\): consumption absent the announcement

  • Target parameter: \[ \alpha_{t^*,t} = \mathbb{E}[C_{t^*,t}(1) - C_{t^*,t}(0)] \]

  • Can we identify this without specifying the full model?

Social Security: Difference-in-Differences

  • Use the cohort aged 65 (unaffected) as a control group
  • Parallel trends: for \(t^*<65\), ages \(t\geq t^*\) and \(s < t^*\): \[ \mathbb{E}[C_{t^*,t}(0)] - \mathbb{E}[C_{65,t}(0)] = \mathbb{E}[C_{t^*,s}(0)] - \mathbb{E}[C_{65,s}] \]
  • The DD estimator constructs the counterfactual from observables: \[ \alpha_{t^*,t} = \mathbb{E}[C_{t^*,t}(1)] - \mathbb{E}[C_{65,t}(0)] - \left(\mathbb{E}[C_{t^*,s}(0)] - \mathbb{E}[C_{65,s}(0)]\right) \]

Social Security: Regression Specification

  • The above was for a specific \((t^*, \text{control cohort}, s)\) triple

  • With many cohorts and ages, the common regression: \[ \mathbb{E}[C_{t^*,t}] = \gamma_{t^*} + \mu_{t} + \alpha_{t^*,t}\mathbf{1}\{t\geq t^*\} \]

  • This layers in a stricter set of parallel trends assumptions

  • No need to specify utility, discount factors, or savings technology

  • Only need to defend parallel trends

  • Farther-apart cohorts make this assumption stronger

  • The regression cannot select “more ideal” controls for each treated cohort (Goodman-Bacon 2021)

Example: Firm Entry and Minimum Wage

  • Panel data \((A_{m,t,1}, A_{m,t,2}, Z_{m,t})\) on firm activity across markets

  • \(A_{m,t,j}\): whether firm \(j\) is active in market \(m\) at \(t\); entry when \(A_{m,t+1,j} - A_{m,t,j} = 1\)

  • \(X_m\): unobservable market-level profitability shifter

  • \(Z \in \{0,1\}\): local minimum wage policy indicator

  • Payoff to entering under the policy: \[ u_{1}(x,a,d') = \phi_{0} + \phi_{1}x - \phi_{2}d' - \phi_{3}(1-a) + \phi_{4}z \]

  • \(\phi_4\) captures the minimum wage effect on firm payoffs

Firm Entry: Target and Identification

  • \(Z_{m,1} = 0\) initially; a subset adopt permanently at \(t^*\), unanticipated
  • Question: “What is the effect of the minimum wage on firm entry?”
  • \(N_{m,t} = D_{m,t,1} + D_{m,t,2}\): number of active firms
  • \(N_{\tau,m}(z)\): potential outcome of \(N\) in market \(m\), \(\tau\) periods after adoption
  • Dynamic treatment effect on the treated: \[ \alpha_{\tau} = \mathbb{E}[N_{\tau}(1) - N_{\tau}(0) \mid Z = 1] \]
  • Effects differ across markets — heterogeneity matters
  • Selection into adoption: \(\mathbb{E}[\Delta_{\tau}|Z=1] \neq \mathbb{E}[\Delta_{\tau}]\)

Firm Entry: Parallel Trends

  • Parallel trends justifying the event study: \[ \mathbb{E}[N_{t}(0)|Z=1] - \mathbb{E}[N_{t}(0)|Z=0] = \text{constant} \]

  • If markets are in the ergodic distribution of the MPE, \(N\) is stationary absent intervention and parallel trends holds

  • Event-study regression: \[ D_{m,t} = \gamma_{m} + \mu_{t} + \mathbf{1}\{t\geq t^*\}\alpha_{t-t^*} + \epsilon_{m,t} \]

  • Robustly identifies the average effect among adopters (uniform timing)

  • With staggered adoption and heterogeneous effects, regression delivers a hard-to-interpret weighted average (Goodman-Bacon 2021)

Example: Tax Reform Experiments

  • Three states run different experiments with taxes and transfers
  • Let \(\mathcal{Y}_j\) be the net income function in state \(j\): \[\begin{align} \mathcal{Y}_{A}(W,H) &= b_{A} + (1-\tau_{A})WH \\ \mathcal{Y}_{B}(W,H) &= WH + \sum_{k=0}^{5}\tau_{k}(WH-\overline{E}_{k})\mathbf{1}\{WH>\overline{E}_{k}\} \\ \mathcal{Y}_{C}(W,H) &= WH(1-\tau_{C}) + \mathbf{1}\{H>20\}b_{C} \end{align}\]
  • Participants don’t anticipate assignment; the experiment is temporary (3 periods)
  • \(Z_j \in \{0,1\}\): assignment to treatment or control in state \(j\)

Tax Reform: Identification

  • Question: “What is the effect of each unannounced, temporary reform on labor supply?”
  • Compare treatment and control means in each state: \[\mathbb{E}[H \mid Z_j = 1, j] - \mathbb{E}[H \mid Z_j = 0, j]\]
  • Random assignment identifies the ATE — no further assumptions needed

Reasons to Use a Model

When the Question Requires One

  • The most obvious reason: the research question cannot be articulated without a model

  • The most useful modeling insights concern efficiency, market failures, and policy design

  • What does the optimal tax and transfer system look like?

  • What is the cheapest way to incentivize competition between firms?

  • What are the welfare costs of incomplete insurance markets?

  • These involve counterfactual policy environments never observed

  • They require specifying objectives, constraints, and behavioral responses

Welfare Calculations

  • Revealed preference: treating individuals as knowing what they like lets us infer preferences and evaluate policy

  • How do individuals value the social security program? How would they value a different \((\tau, b)\)?

  • What is the willingness to pay for a lump-sum transfer \(b\) financed by taxes \(\tau\)? (We return to this below)

Making Sense of Puzzling Data

  • The most fundamental causal question: what is the effect of price on quantities?
  • A silly question — but only because supply and demand theory is so fundamental
  • Without theory, all you see is a cloud of points:

Figure 1: Shifting supply and demand curves trace out equilibrium points

Making Sense of Puzzling Data

  • With supply and demand, each point is the equilibrium outcome of two structural relationships
  • Wright proposed instrumental variables to disentangle supply from demand
  • Perhaps the earliest estimated structural model

When Variation Doesn’t Identify the Counterfactual

  • In the examples above, we defined the “treatment” very specifically
  • Models help articulate what can and cannot be identified by observed variation
  • They outline assumptions under which related counterfactuals can be forecast, even without exact replication
  • Often framed as internal vs external validity — but the “external validity” column is too rich to leave undiscussed

Heckman and Vytlacil (2005)

Estimands from simple statistical models designed to infer causal effects (IV, DD, RD) are rarely parameters of exact policy interest

Social Security: A Concrete Case

To see what DD misses, simplify the savings model:

  • Each individual faces a known income sequence \(\{y_{n,t}\}_{t=1}^{T}\)
  • Agents face a natural borrowing constraint: \[ \sum_{s=t}^{T}q_{s-t}c_{s} \leq a_{t} + \sum_{s=t}^{T}q_{s-t}(1-\tau)y_{n,s} + \sum_{s\geq 65}^{T}q_{s-t}b \] where \(q_{\tau} = 1/(1+r)^{\tau}\) is the price of consumption \(\tau\) periods ahead
  • \(\beta(1+r)=1\), \(\psi = 0\): agents perfectly smooth consumption to NPV of net income

Social Security: Consumption Effect

  • Mean consumption effect for cohort \(t^*\) at any \(t \geq t^*\) equals the NPV effect of the announcement: \[ \Delta C_{t^*,t} = \sum_{s=60}^{64}q_{s-t^*}b + \sum_{s=t^*}^{T}(\tau - \tau')\overline{y}_{t^*,t} \] where \(\overline{y}_{t^*,t}\) is average income for cohort \(t^*\) at age \(t\)
  • Parallel trends holds: cross-cohort differences are constant with age (\(\mu_t\) normalizable to zero)
  • DD robustly identifies \(\alpha_{t^*,t}\)
  • The model implies \(\alpha_{t^*,t}\) is constant in \(t\)
  • Each effect depends on how cohort \(t^*\) expects the expansion to be financed (\(\tau' - \tau\)), which we haven’t specified

Social Security: What DD Doesn’t Tell Us

  • The \(\alpha_{t^*,t}\) are informative but don’t tell us about future policy changes
  • They don’t even tell us what would happen if the expansion were repealed
  • Extrapolation requires more theory

DD does not identify:

  • The total effect of social security (not just the expansion)
  • Effects of additional changes in the eligibility age
  • Effects of changes in \(b\) and \(\tau\) at different ages and anticipation horizons
  • Effects on cohort \(t^*\) if they learned at any age other than \(t^*\) (one-to-one mapping between cohort and treatment)
  • Effects for cohorts who anticipated the policy their whole life
  • Effects under alternative financing arrangements

Tax Reform: What the Model Reveals

  • Effect of reform \(A\) when \(\beta(1+r) = 1\) (stationary consumption): \[\Delta H_{n,t} = \psi\log(1-\tau) - \psi\sigma\Delta \log\left(C^*_{n}\right) \]

  • \(\Delta C^*_n\) embodies the income effect; solved by plugging optimal labor supply into the intertemporal budget constraint

  • The ATE depends on income effects, which depend on the perceived persistence of the reform

  • Substantial heterogeneity in treatment effects

  • Different distributions of wages or work costs across populations imply different impacts

Tax Reform: What Experiments Don’t Tell Us

Each experiment robustly identifies the effect of reform \(j\) on population \(j\). It does not identify:

  • Effects of reforms with different persistence — experimental findings must be interpreted carefully regarding perceived duration
  • The effect of reform \(A\) on population \(B\), or vice versa
  • The distribution of individual treatment effects
  • Effects of partly anticipated reforms
  • Effects at scale, where general equilibrium responses may appear
  • The model can invert out wage and work cost distributions from panel data, then return to the experimental data to estimate ATEs along latent dimensions

Entry-Exit: What the Event Study Doesn’t Tell Us

The model-free approach does not identify:

  • The effect when the minimum wage introduction is anticipated

  • The effect on markets that do not adopt

  • The effect of repealing the minimum wage

  • The effect of nominal changes to the minimum wage

  • Each of these is arguably a more useful or compelling policy calculation

Interpolating Variation

  • Each tax reform is related: each takes the form \(\mathcal{Y}(W,H)\) over wages and hours

  • Estimating labor supply as a nonparametric function of \(\mathcal{Y}\) would require implausible amounts of policy variation

  • Models interpolate related — but not functionally identical — variation

  • In the labor supply model, any \(\mathcal{Y}\) enters through the budget constraint — different reforms are comparable without new parameters

  • See Mullins (2026) for a similar exercise with welfare reform experiments

Articulated Variation

  • Articulated variation: when a policy’s effect can be modeled without additional parameters

  • Typically involves a priori known changes to prices and endowments

  • With structural parameters in hand, any observed variation in \(\mathcal{Y}\) can be interpreted through the model

  • Counterexample: the minimum wage in the entry-exit model

  • Embodied as a reduced-form parameter \(\phi_4\) because within-period production is not modeled

  • The policy is not well-articulated: further nominal wage changes cannot be studied

Marschak’s Maxim

The Principle

  • We’ve explored the boundaries of causal inference and where modeling adds value
  • Less instructive for how to do economic modeling
  • More art than science — but Marschak’s Maxim is a useful guiding principle

Marschak’s Maxim (Marschak 1953; Heckman and Vytlacil 2007)

Researchers should specify the minimal set of model ingredients needed to answer their research question of interest

Marschak’s Maxim: In Practice

  • This may seem obvious — the hard part is figuring out what the minimal set is

  • Surprisingly easy to forget once deep inside your model

  • “Is this ingredient essential to my question?” — ask repeatedly

  • Your research question is the mast you tie yourself to

  • Decide what it is as early as possible

Marschak’s Original Example: Setup

  • A monopolist chooses quantities to maximize profit; the government taxes quantities
  • Demand: \(p = \alpha_0 - \alpha_1 q\)
  • Profits (constant marginal cost \(c\)): \[ \Pi(q) = (\alpha_0 - c - \tau)q - \alpha_1 q^2 \]
  • Optimal quantity and tax revenue: \[q^* = \frac{\alpha_0 - c - \tau}{2\alpha_1}, \qquad R = \frac{(\alpha_0 - c - \tau)\tau}{2\alpha_1}\]

Marschak’s Original Example: Three Problems

Suppose \(q\) varies exogenously across markets. Three problems, three information requirements:

  1. Firm maximizing profit: observe \(\Pi = aq - bq^2\), set \(q^* = a/2b\)
  2. Government forecasting revenue: only needs \(\tau\) and observed quantities
  3. Government optimizing taxes: extract \(a = \alpha_0 - c - \tau\) from the reduced form, set \(\tau^* = (\alpha_0 - c)/2\)
  • The setup is unconventional — we typically assume agents already act optimally

Lessons from Marschak’s Example

Two insights:

  1. Each question requires only certain combinations of parameters, each observable in reduced-form relationships
  2. Once agents optimize, reduced-form relationships no longer suffice for forecasting policy effects
  • Point (2) is the Lucas Jr (1976) critique, articulated two decades earlier
  • Point (1): answers to some questions are invariant to particular model ingredients
  • In practice we can’t always guarantee this, but the insight motivates seeking a minimal set of ingredients

Two-Stage Budgeting

  • Utility over \(K\) commodities \(x\) with price vector \(p\): \[V_t(p,a,y) = \max_{x,a'} \left\{ v(x) + \beta\, \mathbb{E}\, V_{t+1}(p',a',y') \right\}\] subject to: \[ p \cdot x + \frac{1}{1+r}a' \leq y_t,\qquad a \geq \underline{a} \]
  • Suppose the question concerns savings accumulation and intertemporal policy counterfactuals

Two-Stage Budgeting: The Result

  • If \(v\) is homothetic, there exists a price index \(P(p)\) and indirect utility \(u\) such that (Gorman 1959): \[V(p,X) = u(X / P(p))\]

  • Consumers first allocate aggregate expenditure across periods, then allocate within periods separately

  • Classic application of Marschak’s Maxim: if your question is about intertemporal allocation and counterfactuals don’t affect intratemporal margins, no need to model intratemporal consumption under regularity conditions

  • All you need is a reasonable price index

  • Gorman (1959) and others sought assumptions permitting broader expenditure categories in demand systems

Sufficient Statistics: Setup

  • Static labor supply for \(i \in [0,1]\): \(H_i = \arg\max_h\; C - v(h)\), with \(C = b + (1-\tau)hW_i\)
  • Transfer financed through taxes: \(b = \tau \int W_i H_i\, di\)
  • Utilitarian welfare objective: \[ V(\tau) = \int (C_i - v(H_i))\, di \]
  • What is the welfare effect of a marginal expansion in \(b\), financed through \(\tau\)?

Sufficient Statistics: Derivation

  • Applying the envelope theorem: \[ \frac{dV}{d\tau} = -\int W_iH_i\,di + \int W_iH_i\,di + \tau \int \frac{d(W_iH_i)}{d\tau}\, di \]
  • Assuming a constant elasticity of taxable income \(Z_i = W_iH_i\) w.r.t. \(\tau\): \[\frac{dV}{d\tau} = \bar{Z}\; \varepsilon_{Z,\tau}\]
  • The willingness to pay for the transfer exactly cancels the mechanical tax cost
  • Only the distortionary cost of behavioral adjustments remains (Harberger 1954)

Sufficient Statistics: Scope and Limits

  • A larger literature on sufficient statistics in public finance derives approximations to local policy effects

  • Welfare formulas depending on empirical quantities and elasticities

  • Less reliance on specific functional forms

  • As models grow more elaborate, required elasticities can become intractable (Kleven 2021)

  • Sufficient statistics remain a useful guide to understanding policy fundamentals

  • A great example of Marschak’s Maxim!

Summary

Takeaways

  • Quasi-experimental methods are powerful when observed variation directly identifies the counterfactual
  • Models become essential for welfare, optimal policy, or out-of-sample counterfactuals
  • Models interpolate related variation through articulated policy channels — connecting different reforms through the same structural parameters
  • Marschak’s Maxim: always aim for the minimal set of ingredients your question requires
