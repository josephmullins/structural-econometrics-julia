11  The Entry-Exit Model

11.1 Data and Choice Probabilities

Identification arguments begin with a description of the available data. In the entry-exit model, the fundamental object that summarizes the data is the equilibrium probability of entry:

\[ p(x,a,a')\]

where \(a \in \{0,1\}\) is the firm’s own incumbency status, \(a' \in \{0,1\}\) is the competitor’s incumbency status, and \(x\) represents market-level state variables. This probability captures the likelihood of entry (or continued operation) given the current market conditions.

With a sufficiently rich cross-section of markets, we can estimate these choice probabilities non-parametrically by simply computing the fraction of firms that enter or remain active conditional on each configuration of state variables. The question of identification then becomes: can we recover the structural parameters from these choice probabilities?

11.2 Informal Identification

The parameters of the model are identified by how the choice probability \(p(x,a,a')\) varies with the state variables. Recall the specification of payoffs in the model:

\[ u_{1}(x,a,d^{\prime}) = \phi_{0} + \phi_{1}x - \phi_{2}d^\prime - \phi_{3}(1-a) \] \[{u}_{0}(x,a) = \phi_{4}a \]

Each parameter in \(\phi\) governs a distinct source of variation in the choice probability:

  • \(\phi_0\) determines the average level of entry across all markets
  • \(\phi_1\) determines how entry changes with respect to the market characteristic \(x\)
  • \(\phi_2\) captures how entry responds to the competitor’s presence
  • \(\phi_3\) determines entry among non-incumbent firms (the entry cost)
  • \(\phi_4\), by making entry more persistent for incumbents, can fit differential sensitivities to market state variables by incumbency status

A simple counting argument provides intuition for identification: the data object \(p(x,a,a')\) provides variation along four dimensions—the continuous variable \(X\) and the four binary configurations of \((a,a')\). Meanwhile, the model contains five parameters. As long as there is sufficient variation in \(X\) and the binary states, we have enough “equations” to solve for the unknowns.

Note

While counting arguments is useful for building intuition, they do not constitute a formal proof of identification. Rigorous identification arguments for dynamic discrete choice models require tools that we will introduce later in the text, including results on the invertibility of conditional choice probabilities.

11.3 Credibility and the Role of Unobservables

Now suppose that \(X\) represents a market characteristic that can be manipulated by policy—perhaps a subsidy for market entry or a regulatory barrier. The model would allow us to forecast how entry patterns change under counterfactual policies by re-computing equilibrium choice probabilities at different values of \(X\).

Discussion

Would you find such a forecast credible? What concerns might you have about using this model for policy analysis?

A key concern is market-level unobservables. In practice, markets differ in ways that are observed by firms but not by the econometrician. If these unobservables are correlated with the observed state variable \(X\), then variation in entry with respect to \(X\) conflates the causal effect of \(X\) with selection on unobservables.

For example, suppose that markets with high values of \(X\) also tend to have unobservably favorable demand conditions. Firms observe these conditions and incorporate them into their entry decisions. The econometrician, seeing higher entry in high-\(X\) markets, might incorrectly attribute this entirely to the effect of \(X\) rather than to the correlated unobservable.

This is a familiar endogeneity problem, but note that it is more complicated than in the linear regression setting. In the entry-exit model, the unobservable enters the payoff function nonlinearly and affects not only the firm’s own decision but also its beliefs about the competitor’s behavior in equilibrium.

11.4 Two Approaches to Identification

There are two standard approaches to addressing this endogeneity concern:

11.4.1 Instrumental Variables

The first solution is to find an instrument \(Z\) that shifts \(X\) in ways that are independent of market-level unobservables. The logic is analogous to the linear IV case: we isolate variation in \(X\) that is “as good as random” with respect to the unobservable.

However, implementation is more complex than in the linear case. The nonlinearity of discrete choice models means that standard 2SLS formulas do not directly apply.

The key to using instruments for identification in this setting is:

  1. First to identify the underlying choice probabilities; then
  2. Derive moment restrictions using these choice probabilities. This typically involves writing conditions for optimal decisions in terms of parameters and choice probabilities only, then using such derivations to write moment conditions.

This involves more advanced tools that will be covered later in the text.

11.4.2 Panel Data

The second solution exploits panel data—repeated observations of the same markets over time. If market-level unobservables are persistent (captured by market fixed effects), then within-market variation in entry over time can identify the parameters of interest.

The intuition is similar to the fixed effects approach in linear models: by looking at how entry in the same market changes when \(X\) changes, we difference out any time-invariant unobservable. However, the nonlinearity of discrete choice models again creates complications. The standard fixed effects estimator is inconsistent due to the incidental parameters problem, and specialized techniques are required.

Note

Both the IV and panel data approaches for discrete choice models involve technical challenges that go beyond their linear counterparts. We will develop the necessary tools and revisit these identification strategies in later chapters.

11.5 The Whether vs How of Identification

The discussion above illustrates a recurring theme in structural econometrics. The whether of identification—proving that parameters can in principle be recovered from the data—is often straightforward under the maintained assumptions of the model. The how of identification—which sources of variation are used and whether those sources are credible—is typically more important for applied work.

In the entry-exit model, the basic counting argument suggests that parameters are identified from cross-sectional variation in choice probabilities. But this argument implicitly assumes that all variation in \(X\) is valid for identification. Once we acknowledge the possibility of market-level unobservables, we must be more careful about which variation we exploit.

The “how” of identification

Even when a model is point-identified under its maintained assumptions, the credibility of estimates depends on which features of the data drive identification. Thinking carefully about the sources of variation—and whether they are plausibly exogenous—is essential for convincing empirical work.

This perspective connects structural estimation to the broader program of causal inference in econometrics. A structural model provides a framework for interpreting variation in the data, but the quality of that interpretation depends on whether the identifying variation is credible for the research question at hand.
